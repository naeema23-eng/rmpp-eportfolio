Unit 3 – Seminar Reflection: Peer Review Activity

Evaluating research purpose, methodology and evidence

Unit 3 introduced research methodology and the importance of aligning research questions with appropriate research design and methods. During the seminar peer review activity, I critically examined how existing research papers justify their methodological choices and support their conclusions with evidence.

A key learning from this activity was recognising that strong research is not defined solely by technical complexity, but by the clarity with which purpose, method, data collection and analysis are connected. This influenced how I approached my own project planning, encouraging early consideration of feasibility, validity, and ethical responsibility.

The impact of this learning is reflected in the e-Portfolio activity below, where I justify my selected research methods, data collection approach, and required skills for my proposed project.

Unit 3 – e-Portfolio Activity: Research Proposal Review

Considering methods, data collection and skills for my project

Chosen area of interest

My chosen area of interest is the use of machine learning to predict patient deterioration in prehospital emergency care, based on physiological signals such as heart rate, respiratory rate, blood pressure, SpO₂, EtCO₂ and ECG recorded during ambulance transport.

1. Methods that suit my purpose

From this week’s readings, the method that best suits my purpose is a quantitative, data-driven research design, supported by computational modelling and a structured literature review.

  • Quantitative research is appropriate because the project focuses on numerical physiological variables and measurable outcomes (e.g. deterioration vs. no deterioration).
  • Computational modelling (supervised machine learning) is suitable for discovering temporal patterns in time-series vital signs.
  • A literature-based approach supports justified variable selection, model choice, and evaluation strategy.

Together, these methods align with the module’s emphasis on selecting research approaches that match the aims, data type and practical constraints of a project.

2. Data collection methods I would consider

Because collecting primary prehospital physiological data would require complex approvals and is beyond the scope of this module, I would focus on secondary data:

  • Secondary clinical datasets from open repositories containing high-frequency vital signs and labelled outcomes.
  • Public physiological signal databases suitable for modelling deterioration or instability.
  • Literature-based analysis of early warning scores and AI-based risk prediction studies.

In a future project, qualitative methods (e.g. interviews with paramedics) could complement quantitative analysis, but this module focuses on secondary quantitative data and published research.

3. Skills I need to have or develop

  • Technical and analytical skills: supervised ML for time-series data, noise handling, and model evaluation using appropriate metrics.
  • Research design and statistics: understanding bias, validity, reliability, and linking analysis decisions to research questions.
  • Ethical and professional skills: data protection, responsible AI use, and alignment with professional codes such as GDPR and BCS.

I will also continue developing my academic writing to justify research decisions clearly in later units.

Summary

Overall, a quantitative, secondary-data-based study supported by computational modelling and a strong literature review is realistic for this module and aligned with my long-term goal of developing safe, clinically meaningful AI tools for emergency care.

Unit 10 – Research Writing

Research proposal presentation and feedback reflection

Unit 10 focused on research writing and the structure of large academic artefacts such as dissertations. The main learning outcome was understanding how literature, methodology, ethics and evaluation must form a single coherent argument.

Producing the research proposal presentation and transcript strengthened my ability to communicate complex technical ideas clearly and critically, while feedback helped identify areas for further improvement, particularly around human factors and visual explanation.
