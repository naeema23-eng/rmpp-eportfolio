Unit 3 — e-Portfolio Activity: Research Proposal Review

Methods · Data · Skills

Chosen Area of Interest

Predicting patient deterioration in prehospital emergency care using physiological signals and machine-learning models.

1. Suitable Research Methods for My Project

The most appropriate approach is a quantitative, data-driven research design supported by computational modelling and a structured literature review. This aligns with the use of measurable physiological variables and predictive machine-learning algorithms.

  • Quantitative research – to analyse relationships between physiological signals and deterioration.
  • Computational modelling – using ML algorithms as in-silico experiments.
  • Literature-based approach – to refine variables, model choices and ethical considerations.

2. Data Collection Methods I Would Consider

The project will rely primarily on secondary data, as real-time ambulance data collection is impractical and ethically constrained. Suitable data sources include:

  • Secondary datasets from PhysioNet, ICU/ED deterioration datasets and open-access vital sign collections.
  • Public physiological signal databases relevant to early warning systems.
  • Literature-based extraction of predictive variables from published research.

3. Skills Required for the Project

To complete this project effectively, I will need to develop skills in:

  • Machine learning – supervised learning, time-series analysis and model evaluation.
  • Data handling – preprocessing noisy signals, managing missing data and feature engineering.
  • Statistics – understanding variable interactions and significance.
  • Ethics & professional practice – privacy, fairness, transparency and alignment with ACM/BCS codes.
  • Academic research skills – literature synthesis, framing research questions and referencing.

4. Summary Reflection

This activity helped me refine the methodological foundations for my proposed study. A combination of quantitative analysis, secondary data use and computational modelling is appropriate and achievable. The required skills also align with the module’s learning outcomes and support my development as an AI practitioner in healthcare.

Literature Review Outline (Unit 4)

Formative outline · Ready for review

Working Title

Implementing Machine Learning in Medical Diagnosis: Early Prediction of Patient Deterioration from Prehospital Physiological Signals

1. Focus and Aim of the Review

This review examines how machine learning models can analyse prehospital physiological signals— such as heart rate, respiratory rate, SpO₂, EtCO₂, ECG and blood pressure—to predict deterioration before it becomes clinically visible.

2. Significance of the Topic

Prehospital care involves rapid decisions under pressure. Subtle physiological changes may precede visible deterioration, but are difficult to detect manually. Understanding ML literature is essential for developing safer triage systems and supporting ethical AI in emergency medicine.

3. Context and Theoretical Perspective

This review is grounded in biomedical physiology, clinical risk modelling, emergency operational constraints, and professional ethical frameworks (ACM/BCS).

4. Source Selection Strategy

Searches will target PubMed, IEEE Xplore, ScienceDirect and Google Scholar using terms such as “prehospital deterioration prediction”, “ambulance vital signs”, and “AI early warning system”.

5. Planned Structure

  • Introduction
  • Physiological indicators of deterioration
  • ML approaches for clinical prediction
  • Evidence in prehospital/emergency contexts
  • Operational and ethical challenges
  • Strengths and limitations
  • Conclusion and future direction

6. Main Findings (Preliminary Themes)

Physiological biomarkers show early signatures of instability (HRV, shock index, EtCO₂ patterns).

ML models outperform manual assessment in predicting cardiac arrest and respiratory failure.

Ambulance data are noisy but usable when appropriate preprocessing is applied.

Ethical considerations include transparency, fairness, false positives and responsible AI deployment.

7. Strengths and Limitations

Strengths: strong physiological evidence, growing AI capabilities. Limitations: limited prehospital datasets, lack of external validation, insufficient ethical integration.

8. Discrepancies

Studies disagree on which vital signs are most predictive and on the relative performance of scoring systems vs. ML.

9. Conclusion

ML has substantial potential in early deterioration prediction, but future research must prioritise prehospital datasets, operational realism and ethical safeguards.
