Unit 3 – Seminar Reflection: Peer Review Activity

Evaluating research purpose, design and methods

Unit 3 introduced research methodology and highlighted how methodological choices are shaped by underlying assumptions about knowledge, reality and values. During the seminar peer review activity, I critically evaluated existing research papers to assess how well their research questions aligned with their chosen methods and data collection strategies.

This activity helped me recognise that strong research is not defined solely by the complexity of the method used, but by the clarity with which the research purpose, design, data and analysis are connected. It influenced my own project planning by encouraging earlier consideration of feasibility, validity, and ethical responsibility.

Unit 3 – e-Portfolio Activity: Research Proposal Review

Considering methods, data collection and skills for my project

Chosen area of interest

My chosen area of interest is the use of machine learning to predict patient deterioration in prehospital emergency care, based on physiological signals such as heart rate, respiratory rate, blood pressure, SpO₂, EtCO₂ and ECG recorded during ambulance transport.

1. Methods that suit my purpose

From this week’s readings, the method that best suits my purpose is a quantitative, data-driven research design, supported by computational modelling and a structured literature review.

  • Quantitative research is appropriate due to numerical physiological variables and measurable outcomes.
  • Computational modelling supports pattern discovery in time-series vital signs.
  • A literature-based approach guides justified methodological choices.

2. Data collection methods

  • Secondary clinical datasets from open repositories.
  • Public physiological signal databases.
  • Document and literature analysis of prior research.

3. Skills to develop

  • Machine learning and time-series analysis.
  • Research design and statistical reasoning.
  • Ethical and professional responsibility.

Unit 10 – Research Writing

Research proposal presentation and feedback reflection

Unit 10 focused on research writing and the structure of large academic artefacts such as research proposals and dissertations. A key learning outcome was understanding that effective research writing is not simply descriptive, but involves constructing a coherent argument that integrates the research problem, literature, methodology, ethical considerations and evaluation strategy.

Producing the research proposal presentation and transcript required me to apply these principles in practice. I had to translate complex clinical and machine-learning concepts into a clear, logically structured narrative suitable for both technical and non-technical audiences.

Feedback on the proposal confirmed strengths in clarity, structure and critical reasoning, particularly in identifying limitations of static early warning scores in dynamic ambulance environments. It also highlighted areas for improvement, including greater emphasis on human factors (such as paramedic response to AI alerts) and clearer visual explanation of the data-analysis pipeline.

This learning will directly inform my future dissertation writing, where careful argumentation, transparency of limitations and ethical framing will be essential.
