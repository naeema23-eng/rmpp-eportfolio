Unit 3 – Seminar Reflection: Peer Review Activity

Evaluating research purpose, methodology and evidence

Unit 3 introduced research methodology and highlighted how methodological choices are shaped by ontological, epistemological and axiological assumptions. During the seminar peer review activity, I evaluated published research papers to assess how well their research questions aligned with their chosen methods, data collection strategies and analysis techniques.

This activity reinforced that strong research is defined by coherence rather than complexity. I became more aware of how feasibility, bias, validity and ethical responsibility must be considered early in the research design process. These insights directly influenced how I structured and justified my own project methodology.

Unit 3 – e-Portfolio Activity: Research Proposal Review

Considering methods, data collection and skills for my project

Chosen area of interest

My chosen area of interest is the use of machine learning to predict patient deterioration in prehospital emergency care, using physiological signals such as heart rate, respiratory rate, blood pressure, SpO₂, EtCO₂ and ECG recorded during ambulance transport.

1. Methods that suit my purpose

A quantitative, data-driven research design supported by computational modelling and a structured literature review is most suitable for this project.

  • Quantitative research enables analysis of numerical physiological data.
  • Supervised machine learning supports discovery of temporal deterioration patterns.
  • Literature-based justification guides model and evaluation choices.

2. Data collection methods

  • Secondary clinical datasets from open repositories.
  • Public physiological signal databases.
  • Published studies on early warning scores and ML-based risk prediction.

3. Skills to develop

  • Time-series machine learning and noise handling.
  • Statistical reasoning and research design.
  • Ethical and professional responsibility in healthcare AI.

This activity provided the methodological foundation for later units, including the Literature Review (Unit 7) and the Research Proposal (Unit 10).

Unit 4 – Case Studies, Focus Groups and Observations

Qualitative data collection methods

Unit 4 introduced qualitative data collection methods, including case studies, focus groups and observation. The key learning was understanding how these methods are used to explore complex phenomena and the limitations they introduce in terms of bias, representativeness and generalisability.

For my project, these methods are not suitable as primary data collection tools, as my research focuses on numerical physiological time-series data and measurable deterioration outcomes. However, this unit helped me recognise how qualitative approaches could complement quantitative models in future work.

For example, focus groups or observational studies involving paramedics could help explore how AI-generated alerts are interpreted in practice, and how factors such as workload, trust and alarm fatigue affect real-world use.

This critical evaluation strengthened my ability to justify the exclusion of certain methods while acknowledging their value for ethical and professional insight.

Unit 5 – Interviews, Surveys and Questionnaire Design

Data collection ethics and instrument design

Unit 5 focused on interviews, surveys and questionnaire design, highlighting the importance of question structure, sampling, response bias and ethical responsibility. The unit also introduced pre- and post-testing to evaluate change over time.

For my project, interviews and surveys are not the primary data source, as the investigation centres on physiological measurements rather than self-reported data. However, this unit was critical in developing my awareness of ethical risks associated with data collection.

Case studies such as Cambridge Analytica demonstrated how surveys can be misused, reinforcing the importance of consent, transparency and governance. This informed how I approached data reuse and ethical framing in my proposal.

In future research, structured questionnaires could be used to assess clinician trust and usability of AI-supported decision tools, complementing quantitative performance metrics.

Unit 8 – Data Analysis and Visualisation

Interpreting and presenting results

Unit 8 shifted focus from data collection to data analysis and visualisation. The unit explored differences between qualitative and quantitative analysis, including coding, statistical interpretation and the role of visualisation in communicating findings.

This unit reinforced that analysis does not end with generating results. Poorly designed charts or selective visual emphasis can distort meaning and mislead interpretation. These lessons were particularly relevant when considering performance metrics for machine-learning models.

The proposal outline developed during this unit provided a structured foundation for the full Research Proposal Presentation completed in Unit 10, especially in how results, limitations and evaluation metrics were explained clearly and responsibly.

Unit 10 – Research Writing

Research proposal presentation and feedback reflection

Unit 10 focused on research writing and the structure of large academic artefacts such as research proposals and dissertations. A key learning outcome was understanding that effective research writing involves constructing a coherent argument that integrates the research problem, literature, methodology, ethics and evaluation.

Producing the research proposal presentation and transcript required me to translate complex clinical and machine-learning concepts into a clear, logically structured narrative suitable for both technical and non-technical audiences.

Feedback confirmed strengths in clarity, structure and critical reasoning, particularly in identifying the limitations of static early warning scores in dynamic ambulance environments. It also highlighted areas for improvement, including greater emphasis on human factors and clearer visual explanation of the data-analysis pipeline.

This learning will directly inform my future dissertation writing, where transparency, ethical framing and careful interpretation will be essential.
