Literature Review Outline (Unit 4)

Formative outline · Ready for review

Working Title

Implementing Machine Learning in Medical Diagnosis: Early Prediction of Patient Deterioration from Prehospital Physiological Signals

1. Focus and Aim of the Review

The aim of this literature review is to examine how machine learning models can analyse prehospital physiological signals—such as heart rate, respiratory rate, SpO₂, EtCO₂, ECG and blood pressure—to predict early patient deterioration before it becomes clinically visible. The review is intended for an academic audience studying AI in healthcare and for professionals interested in safe and effective prehospital decision-support systems.

2. Significance of the Topic

Prehospital care requires rapid decision-making under pressure. Research suggests that subtle changes in physiological signals can precede visible deterioration, yet these patterns are difficult to detect manually. Machine learning models have demonstrated strong predictive potential, but prehospital environments present unique challenges such as noise, motion artefacts and incomplete data. Understanding the literature is essential for improving ambulance triage, enhancing patient safety and supporting the ethical integration of AI in emergency medicine.

3. Context and Theoretical Perspective

This review takes a physiology-driven and clinical risk modelling perspective. It draws on frameworks from biomedical physiology, AI for health risk prediction, prehospital operational constraints, and ethical and professional requirements such as fairness, safety, transparency and accountability under ACM and BCS codes.

4. Source Selection Strategy

Searches will be conducted in PubMed, IEEE Xplore, ScienceDirect, Google Scholar and emergency medicine journals. Keywords include: “prehospital deterioration prediction”, “ambulance vital signs”, “physiological signals machine learning”, “AI early warning system”, and “paramedic decision support”.

Inclusion criteria: peer-reviewed studies, recent (last 10–12 years), ML models using physiological data, emergency/prehospital or acute-care focus.

Exclusion criteria: chronic disease prediction, studies without ML, research using only non-physiological data.

5. Planned Structure of the Review

  • Introduction
  • Physiological indicators of early deterioration
  • Machine learning approaches for clinical prediction
  • Evidence from prehospital and emergency care
  • Operational and ethical challenges
  • Strengths, gaps and limitations of the literature
  • Conclusion and suggested research direction

6. Main Findings (Preliminary Themes)

Theme 1: Physiological Signals as Early Deterioration Predictors
Vital signs such as HR variability, SpO₂ patterns, shock index, EtCO₂ and ECG abnormalities show measurable changes before clinical decline, supporting a physiology-centred approach.

Theme 2: ML Outperforms Manual Assessment in Early Prediction
Studies show ML models can forecast cardiac arrest, respiratory failure and haemodynamic collapse earlier than traditional scoring systems or clinician assessment.

Theme 3: Ambulance Data Are Noisy but Usable
Motion artefacts, missing data and short monitoring windows are common, but ML remains effective with proper preprocessing and feature engineering.

Theme 4: Ethical and Professional Considerations
Bias, fairness, false alarms, transparency and data governance are central concerns. Integration must align with ACM/BCS expectations around competence, public safety and privacy.

7. Strengths and Limitations of Existing Literature

Strengths include strong evidence for key biomarkers and promising ML performance. Limitations include a shortage of prehospital-specific datasets, limited multi-parameter models, insufficient attention to ethical deployment and lack of external validation.

8. Discrepancies in the Literature

Studies disagree on which physiological signals are most predictive (e.g., SpO₂ reliability in motion-heavy environments). Some show scoring systems outperforming ML baselines while others show the opposite, revealing inconsistencies in model evaluation.

9. Conclusion and Research Direction

The literature indicates strong potential for ML-driven deterioration prediction using physiological signals, but highlights significant gaps: insufficient prehospital datasets, lack of ethical integration, and limited research on paramedic-AI interaction. Future research should develop physiologically informed and ethically governed early warning systems for ambulance environments.
