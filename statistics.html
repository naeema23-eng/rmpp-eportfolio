Unit 6 – Descriptive Statistics and Levels of Measurement

Unit 6 · Understanding quantitative methods

Unit 6 introduced quantitative methods as a way of examining relationships between variables that are measured numerically and organised into datasets. A dataset can be thought of as a table where each row represents an observation and each column represents a variable of interest. Before selecting any analytical method, it is essential to understand the level of measurement of each variable—nominal, ordinal, interval or ratio—as this determines which statistical techniques are valid.

The focus of this unit was on descriptive statistics. Measures of location, such as the mean and median, describe a “typical” observation, while measures of spread, such as the range, variance and standard deviation, provide insight into the variability or stability of the data. These concepts form the foundation for later inferential techniques.

In relation to my project on predicting patient deterioration in ambulance settings, descriptive statistics are crucial for understanding the baseline characteristics of physiological data. For example, summarising typical heart rate or blood pressure values for stable vs. deteriorating patients helps identify early differences and potential predictive markers. Examining the spread of values also reveals patterns, outliers and inconsistencies that may affect model performance.

This unit reinforced that effective quantitative research begins with clean data, correct understanding of measurement levels, and careful descriptive analysis before advancing to more complex modelling.

Unit 7 – Summary Measures and Hypothesis Testing

Unit 7 · Applying descriptive and inferential statistics

Unit 7 built directly on the descriptive statistics from Unit 6 and moved into inferential statistics. The focus was on understanding how we can use sample data to make statements about a wider population, quantify uncertainty using probability, and test claims through formal hypothesis testing.

The worksheets for this unit used several real-world datasets (B–G) covering weight-loss interventions, income differences, brand preferences, ecological prevalence, packaging designs and filtration agents. For each dataset, I calculated appropriate summary measures (for example, means and standard deviations), selected a suitable statistical test (independent t-test, paired t-test or chi-square), and interpreted the results in context.

The activity showed how descriptive statistics and hypothesis tests work together: summary measures help to understand patterns in the data, while inferential methods indicate whether those patterns are likely to reflect genuine population differences or may be due to random variation. The interpretations below document the key findings for each dataset and link them back to good research practice.

Dataset B – Diets (Summary Measures & Independent t-test)

Unit 7 · e-Portfolio Activity 7.1 & 7.2

Context and variables

Dataset B contains weight-loss data for 100 individuals who followed one of two diets, A or B. The variables are:

  • Diet – the diet undertaken (A or B).
  • Wtloss – weight loss in kilograms after a fixed period (negative values indicate weight gain).

Descriptive statistics

Each diet group contained 50 participants. The mean weight loss on Diet A was approximately 5.341 kg (SD ≈ 2.536), while the mean on Diet B was around 3.710 kg (SD ≈ 2.769). This suggests that Diet A produced greater average weight loss, but both groups showed noticeable variability, with some individuals even gaining weight.

Hypothesis test and interpretation

To determine whether this difference is statistically significant, I used an independent-samples t-test (equal variances supported by an F-test):

  • H₀: There is no difference in mean weight loss between Diet A and Diet B (μA = μB).
  • H₁: The mean weight loss differs between the two diets (μA ≠ μB).

The test produced a t-statistic of approximately t = 3.072 with 98 degrees of freedom and a two-tailed p-value ≈ 0.00275. At the 5% significance level, this allowed me to reject the null hypothesis. Statistically, there is strong evidence that the population mean weight loss differs between the diets, with Diet A performing better in this sample.

Learning points

This exercise showed how descriptive statistics can highlight potential differences, but hypothesis testing is required to judge whether those differences are likely to be real rather than random. It also reinforced key ideas such as setting up H₀ and H₁, using an appropriate test for independent groups, and interpreting p-values in the context of the research question. In my own work with physiological data, I can apply the same logic to compare, for example, the performance of different early-warning scores or machine-learning models.

Dataset C – Superplus (Income by Sex)

Unit 7 · Independent t-test with ratio data

Context and variables

Dataset C records the annual incomes of 120 cardholders of a “Superplus Diamond” card:

  • Sex – male (M) or female (F).
  • Income – gross annual income in thousands of pounds (£000s).

Descriptive statistics

The sample contained 60 males and 60 females. The mean male income was about £52.91k (SD ≈ £15.27k), while the mean female income was roughly £44.23k (SD ≈ £13.79k). On average, male cardholders in this sample earn more, but there is substantial overlap between the two distributions, which is why inferential analysis is needed.

Hypothesis test and interpretation

I used an independent-samples t-test to compare mean incomes:

  • H₀: Mean income is equal for males and females (μM = μF).
  • H₁: Mean income differs by sex (μM ≠ μF).

The test gave t ≈ 3.27 with 118 degrees of freedom and a two-tailed p-value ≈ 0.0014. With p < 0.05, I rejected the null hypothesis. There is strong statistical evidence that male and female mean incomes differ in the population, with higher average income for men in this dataset.

Learning points

This example emphasised that even when distributions overlap, statistically significant differences can exist at the population level. It also illustrated how to work with ratio-level financial data and interpret the results carefully, avoiding over-generalisation. For my future research with AI models, similar tests can be used to compare performance across subgroups (for example, different hospital sites or patient cohorts) to check for fairness and robustness.

Dataset D – Brand Preferences

Unit 7 · Categorical data and Chi-square

Context and variables

Dataset D comes from a marketing study that recorded which brand consumers preferred in two demographic areas. The variables are:

  • Area – demographic area (1 or 2).
  • Brand – preferred brand (A, B or Other).

Descriptive summary

A cross-tabulation by area showed some differences in raw counts, so a Chi-square test was used to check whether any differences were statistically meaningful.

Chi-square test of independence

  • H₀: Brand preference is independent of area.
  • H₁: Brand preference is associated with area.

Using the 2×3 contingency table (Area × Brand), the Chi-square statistic was approximately χ² ≈ 3.29 with 2 degrees of freedom, giving a p-value ≈ 0.193. Because p > 0.05, I failed to reject the null hypothesis. Statistically, there is not enough evidence to say that brand preference differs significantly between the two areas.

Learning points

This dataset helped me practise working with purely categorical data and reinforced when to use a Chi-square test. It also highlighted the difference between apparent differences in raw counts and statistically significant differences. In AI and healthcare, similar contingency-table approaches can be applied to outcomes such as “deteriorated vs. stable” across different hospitals or triage categories to test whether patterns are random or meaningful.

Dataset E – Heather Prevalence

Unit 7 · Frequency tables and association

Context and variables

Dataset E summarises an ecological study of heather prevalence across two heathland locations. The data are already collated into a frequency table:

  • Location – A or B.
  • Prevalence – Absent, Sparse or Abundant.

Frequency distribution and interpretation

The frequency table showed how many transects fell into each prevalence category at each location, which is a useful way to summarise discrete categorical outcomes before any modelling. Patterns in the table suggest that prevalence varies between locations, likely reflecting different habitat conditions.

Learning points

This example strengthened my ability to interpret frequency tables and think carefully about how categorical outcomes can be compared across groups. In healthcare and AI research, similar summaries are used for deterioration categories, triage levels, or model output classes before applying inferential tests or predictive modelling.

Dataset F – Container Designs (Paired t-test)

Unit 7 · Comparing two related conditions

Context and variables

Dataset F comes from a detergent manufacturer that tested two different container designs. Each store sold the product in both designs and the number of items sold was recorded:

  • Store – store ID (1–10).
  • Con1 – items sold in Container Design 1.
  • Con2 – items sold in Container Design 2.

Descriptive statistics

Across the ten stores, the mean number of units sold was about 172.6 for Design 1 and 159.4 for Design 2. Because each store is measured under both conditions, the observations are paired, and the key quantity is the difference in sales per store (Con1 − Con2). The mean difference was approximately +13.2 units.

Paired-samples t-test

  • H₀: The mean difference in sales between designs is zero.
  • H₁: The mean difference in sales is not zero.

The test gave t ≈ 2.875 with 9 degrees of freedom and a two-tailed p-value ≈ 0.0183. With p < 0.05, I rejected the null hypothesis. The evidence suggests that sales under Design 1 and Design 2 differ significantly, with Design 1 performing better in this sample.

Dataset G – Filtration Agents (Paired t-test)

Unit 7 · Paired design and process quality

Context and variables

Dataset G relates to the final process in the production of a chemical product. Each batch is filtered using two different agents, and the remaining impurities are measured:

  • Batch – batch ID (1–12).
  • Agent1 – impurities after filtration with Agent 1 (parts per 1000).
  • Agent2 – impurities after filtration with Agent 2 (parts per 1000).

Lower impurity values indicate better filtration performance.

Descriptive statistics

On average, impurity levels were approximately 8.25 parts per 1000 for Agent 1 and 8.68 for Agent 2. Because each batch was processed with both agents, the data are paired. The mean difference (Agent1 − Agent2) was about −0.433, indicating that impurity levels tended to be lower—and therefore better—for Agent 1.

Paired-samples t-test

  • H₀: There is no difference in mean impurity between Agent 1 and Agent 2.
  • H₁: There is a difference in mean impurity between the two agents.

The results gave t ≈ −3.264 with 11 degrees of freedom and a two-tailed p-value ≈ 0.00755. Since p < 0.05, I rejected the null hypothesis. Statistically, there is strong evidence that the agents differ, and the negative mean difference suggests that Agent 1 leaves fewer impurities and is therefore more effective in this sample.
