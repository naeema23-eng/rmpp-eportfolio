Unit 6 – Descriptive Statistics and Levels of Measurement

Unit 6 · Understanding quantitative methods

Unit 6 introduced quantitative methods as a way of examining relationships between variables that are measured numerically and organised into datasets. A dataset can be thought of as a table where each row represents an observation and each column represents a variable of interest. Before selecting any analytical method, it is essential to understand the level of measurement of each variable—nominal, ordinal, interval or ratio—as this determines which statistical techniques are valid.

The focus of this unit was on descriptive statistics. Measures of location, such as the mean and median, describe a “typical” observation, while measures of spread, such as the range, variance and standard deviation, provide insight into the variability or stability of the data. These concepts form the foundation for later inferential techniques.

In relation to my project on predicting patient deterioration in ambulance settings, descriptive statistics are crucial for understanding the baseline characteristics of physiological data. For example, summarising typical heart rate or blood pressure values for stable vs. deteriorating patients helps identify early differences and potential predictive markers. Examining the spread of values also reveals patterns, outliers and inconsistencies that may affect model performance.

This unit reinforced that effective quantitative research begins with clean data, correct understanding of measurement levels, and careful descriptive analysis before advancing to more complex modelling.

Unit 7 – Inferential Statistics: Worksheets & Interpretation

Unit 7 · Summary measures, hypothesis testing, and evidence-based conclusions

Unit 7 built on descriptive statistics and introduced inferential statistics and hypothesis testing. The key learning was that conclusions about a wider population are made from sample data under uncertainty. This requires correctly selecting tests (independent vs. paired), understanding assumptions (such as equal variances), and interpreting p-values as evidence rather than “proof”.

Below, I document my completed worksheet outputs (7.1 Summary Measures and 7.2 Hypothesis Testing), using the provided datasets. Each card includes a concise interpretation to demonstrate understanding of the results.

Dataset B · Measures of location and spread

Key results (Diet B)

  • Mean: 3.710
  • Standard deviation: 2.769
  • Median: 3.745
  • Minimum: −4.148
  • Maximum: 10.539
  • Q1: 1.953
  • Q3: 5.4035

Interpretation

Diet B shows a moderate average weight loss but relatively high variability. While some participants experienced substantial weight reduction, others showed minimal or negative outcomes. The spread (SD and IQR) suggests meaningful individual differences in response, which supports the need for inferential testing when comparing Diet A vs. Diet B.

Unit 7.2 – Hypothesis Testing (Dataset B: Diets)

Dataset B · F-test (variance) + independent t-test (two-tailed)

Results

  • Variance check (F-test p-value): 0.5399
  • Independent t-test p-value (equal variances, two-tailed): 0.00275

Conclusion

Since p < 0.05 for the independent t-test, the null hypothesis is rejected. There is a statistically significant difference in mean weight loss between Diet A and Diet B. The variance test result (p > 0.05) supports the use of an equal-variance t-test in this comparison.

Unit 7.2 – Paired t-test (Dataset F: Designs)

Dataset F · Comparing two container designs across the same stores

Result

  • Paired t-test p-value (two-tailed): 0.01834

Interpretation

The paired t-test indicates a statistically significant difference between the two container designs (p < 0.05). Because the measurements are taken from the same stores under both conditions, pairing is appropriate and helps control for store-to-store variability.

Unit 7.2 – Paired t-test (Dataset G: Filtration)

Dataset G · Comparing impurity levels for Agent 1 vs Agent 2 across the same batches

Result

  • Paired t-test p-value (two-tailed): 0.00755

Interpretation

There is strong evidence that impurity levels differ between the two filtration agents (p < 0.05). In this dataset, the paired structure (same batches tested under both agents) supports a paired t-test. The result suggests Agent 1 is associated with lower mean impurity levels.
