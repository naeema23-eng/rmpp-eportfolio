Unit 6 – Descriptive Statistics and Levels of Measurement

Unit 6 · Understanding quantitative methods

Unit 6 introduced quantitative methods as a way of examining relationships between variables that are measured numerically and organised into datasets. A dataset can be thought of as a table where each row represents an observation and each column represents a variable of interest. Before selecting any analytical method, it is essential to understand the level of measurement of each variable—nominal, ordinal, interval or ratio—as this determines which statistical techniques are valid.

The focus of this unit was on descriptive statistics. Measures of location, such as the mean and median, describe a “typical” observation, while measures of spread, such as the range, variance and standard deviation, provide insight into the variability or stability of the data. These concepts form the foundation for later inferential techniques.

In relation to my project on predicting patient deterioration in ambulance settings, descriptive statistics are crucial for understanding the baseline characteristics of physiological data. For example, summarising typical heart rate or blood pressure values for stable vs. deteriorating patients helps identify early differences and potential predictive markers. Examining the spread of values also reveals patterns, outliers and inconsistencies that may affect model performance.

This unit reinforced that effective quantitative research begins with clean data, correct understanding of measurement levels, and careful descriptive analysis before advancing to more complex modelling.

Unit 7 – Inferential Statistics & Hypothesis Testing (Worksheets)

Unit 7 · Inference, probability, hypothesis tests

Unit 7 introduced inferential statistics as the process of making evidence-based conclusions about a population using a sample, while recognising uncertainty and quantifying it using probability. The main practical focus was applying hypothesis testing to determine whether observed differences are likely to reflect real effects rather than random variation.

The worksheet calculations were completed in Excel. The results below summarise the outputs (summary measures, variance check where applicable, and hypothesis test p-values) and provide brief interpretation aligned to the questions in the Unit 7 exercises.

Unit 8 – Data Analysis and Visualisation

Unit 8 · Charts, distributions, and ethical data presentation

Overview

Unit 8 focused on analysing and presenting quantitative data using appropriate visualisation techniques, while also developing an awareness of how data presentation can influence interpretation and decision-making. Through a series of structured Excel exercises, I explored frequency distributions, percentage comparisons, and histogram construction to identify patterns within datasets and communicate findings clearly and ethically.

Interpretation of Frequency and Percentage Charts

Single-area frequency and percentage analysis (Exercise 8.1D)

The frequency and percentage charts for categories A, B, and Other provide a clear overview of distribution within a single area. The charts show that the “Other” category dominates both in absolute frequency and relative percentage, while categories A and B represent smaller but distinct proportions of the dataset. Presenting both frequencies and percentages is essential, as frequencies show the raw scale of observations, whereas percentages allow for proportional comparison independent of sample size.

This exercise highlights the importance of selecting the appropriate visual representation depending on the analytical goal. While frequency charts are useful for understanding volume, percentage charts are more effective when comparing relative importance between categories. Using both together avoids misinterpretation that could arise if only one representation were presented.

Comparative analysis across areas and locations (Exercises 8.2D and 8.2E)

The clustered bar charts comparing Area 1 and Area 2, and Location A and Location B, demonstrate how visualisation supports comparative analysis. In these charts, differences between categories become immediately apparent. For example, some categories show higher frequencies in one area or location compared to another, indicating potential contextual or environmental influences on the observed outcomes.

The percentage charts are particularly valuable in this context, as they normalise the data and allow meaningful comparison even when total sample sizes differ between groups. Without percentage-based visualisation, conclusions drawn from raw frequencies alone could be misleading. This reinforces the principle that data visualisation is not merely decorative, but a critical analytical tool that shapes how findings are understood.

Histogram Analysis and Distribution Shape (Exercise 8.3B)

The histogram constructed for Diet A illustrates the distribution of weight loss values across defined class intervals. By grouping the data into five equal-width classes and plotting relative frequency against class marks, the histogram reveals the overall shape of the distribution. The results indicate that most observations fall within the middle class intervals, with fewer values at the lower and upper extremes.

This pattern suggests a concentration of outcomes around moderate weight loss values, with limited extreme responses. The histogram provides insights that would not be visible from summary statistics alone, such as the mean and standard deviation. In particular, it allows assessment of skewness, spread, and clustering, which are essential for understanding variability and potential outliers within the dataset.

Ethical Considerations in Data Visualisation

A key theme in Unit 8 was the ethical responsibility of the analyst when presenting data. Visualisations can be manipulated, intentionally or unintentionally, through choices such as axis scaling, category inclusion, or selective reporting of results. For example, including inappropriate categories (such as totals within frequency charts) or choosing misleading scales could exaggerate or minimise apparent differences.

The case study discussion reinforced that even when data values are correct, presenting analyses in a way that supports only a preferred conclusion undermines professional integrity. As a computing and data professional, it is essential to present results transparently, acknowledge limitations, and avoid techniques that could mislead stakeholders. Ethical data visualisation is therefore a fundamental component of responsible research practice.

These skills will also be directly applicable in professional contexts where data-driven decisions must be justified clearly and ethically.

Unit 9 – Validity, Reliability and Generalisability

Unit 9 · Research quality and ethical interpretation

Overview

Unit 9 focused on three core dimensions of research quality: validity, reliability, and generalisability. These concepts are essential for judging whether conclusions are trustworthy, whether statistical findings can be interpreted correctly, and whether results can be applied beyond the immediate study sample. In practice, even technically correct analysis can become misleading if reliability is weak, validity is compromised by bias or confounding, or generalisability is overstated.

Validity and Reliability

Reliability refers to consistency: if the same measurement process is repeated under the same conditions, it should yield similar results. Reliability concerns include measurement error, inconsistent data collection procedures, and analyst-dependent steps that cannot be reproduced.

Validity refers to accuracy of meaning: whether a measure or method truly captures the construct being studied. Validity can be considered across multiple forms. Internal validity concerns whether observed effects are truly due to the factor of interest rather than confounders. Construct validity concerns whether the chosen variables and operational definitions genuinely represent the theoretical concept. Inferential validity concerns whether statistical conclusions follow appropriately from the data and chosen tests.

A key learning point is that reliability is a prerequisite for validity: a method that produces inconsistent outcomes cannot be trusted to measure the intended construct accurately. In addition, practices such as selective reporting or “cherry-picking” statistically favourable results can undermine inferential validity by presenting only part of the evidential picture.

Generalisability

Generalisability concerns whether findings apply beyond the sample studied. In quantitative research, generalisability depends on sampling strategy, sample size, representativeness, and whether the study context matches the context where results will be applied. Even statistically significant results may not generalise if the sample is biased or highly specific.

In qualitative research, the aim is often depth rather than population inference. Therefore, transferability is achieved through rich contextual description and transparent reasoning rather than statistical representativeness. Across both approaches, the key ethical responsibility is to avoid overstating claims beyond what the study design can support.

Connection to My MSc Project Context

These concepts are directly relevant to my ongoing interest in predicting patient deterioration in ambulance settings. If a predictive model is trained on physiological data collected under specific operational conditions, the model’s performance may not generalise to different regions, different ambulance crews, or different patient populations. Ensuring reliability requires consistent data acquisition protocols. Ensuring validity requires careful definition of deterioration outcomes, awareness of confounding factors (e.g., medication, comorbidities), and transparent evaluation. Ensuring generalisability requires testing across varied settings and reporting limitations clearly.
