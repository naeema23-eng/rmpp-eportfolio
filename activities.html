Seminar 2: Peer Review Activity

Unit 3 · Evaluating literature, research design and methods

In this seminar, I reviewed two research papers in the broad area of AI in healthcare that used different research methods. One paper applied a quantitative machine-learning approach to predict patient deterioration using physiological time-series data, while the second paper used a qualitative, interview-based study to explore clinicians’ experiences with AI-assisted early warning systems.

Purpose and contribution

Both papers contributed to the body of knowledge in clinical decision support. The quantitative paper aligned closely with my interest in machine-learning models built on vital-sign patterns, while the qualitative paper highlighted adoption challenges such as trust, alarm fatigue and workflow constraints.

Appropriateness of methods

The quantitative paper used supervised learning and appropriate evaluation metrics, making it suitable for a prediction-focused research question. The qualitative paper used semi-structured interviews and thematic analysis, which was ideal for exploring human factors and clinician perspectives.

Data collection and analysis

The quantitative study relied on secondary datasets with high-frequency vital signs and followed rigorous validation steps. The qualitative study used carefully coded interview transcripts to generate themes. Both approaches fit the nature of their research aims.

Evidence and claims

In both papers, claims were supported with appropriate evidence — performance results and comparisons in the quantitative study, and representative quotations and consistent thematic patterns in the qualitative study.

Suggestions for enhancement

  • Add external validation for the ML model using prehospital datasets.
  • Include explainability techniques (SHAP, LIME) to support clinical interpretability.
  • Extend the qualitative study to include paramedics and EMS context.

Reflection

This activity strengthened my understanding of how different research methods work together. For my own topic — predicting patient deterioration in prehospital care — a quantitative ML approach will be central, but qualitative insights about real-world usage remain valuable. This activity supports LO3 by helping me critically evaluate how existing studies are structured.

Seminar 3: Case Study on Privacy

Unit 4 · Ethical and legal issues in data access

This seminar focused on a privacy case involving Ricardo, a records clerk with access to sensitive property tax data, and Beth, a researcher requesting identifiable information that she was not authorised to access. The scenario highlighted important questions about consent, responsible data handling and organisational policy.

Ethical issues for Ricardo

If Ricardo is not responsible for determining access, he must not release any identifiable data. He is bound by organisational policy, confidentiality obligations and data protection regulations. Even if he is responsible for access decisions, he must evaluate whether the request adheres to lawful, ethical and professional standards.

Ethical issues for the Records Department

The department must consider whether releasing names aligns with the original purpose of the data collection, whether Beth has ethical approval, and whether disclosure poses risks to the individuals. Issues of re-identification, privacy, fairness and public trust are central.

Should Beth contact the individuals?

Beth should only contact individuals if informed consent is obtained through an approved ethical process. Having access to anonymised data does not justify retrieving or using identifiable information unless the organisation explicitly authorises it and privacy safeguards are in place.

Non-response and study validity

If only one-third give permission, one-third deny and one-third do not respond, Beth cannot assume implied consent from non-responders. Her options include:

  • Proceeding with only the consenting group.
  • Redesigning the methodology.
  • Using anonymised or aggregated data instead.
  • Providing a methodological justification for the smaller sample size.

Reflection

This case connects directly to my own experience in healthcare, where physiological and clinical data must be handled with extreme care. Ethical approval, consent and data minimisation are essential before using any identifiable data. The seminar reinforced the importance of responsible practice as outlined across ACM and BCS ethical codes.

Wiki Activity: Questionnaires

Unit 5 · Designing surveys responsibly

For the wiki activity, I selected a publicly available healthcare technology adoption questionnaire aimed at clinicians. At first glance, the layout appeared professional, but a closer review revealed several design problems that could seriously affect data quality.

First, some items were double-barrelled. For example, one question asked how confident respondents were that AI tools would “improve patient outcomes and support daily workflow”. These are two different constructs, and combining them in a single question makes it impossible to know which aspect the respondent is rating. Second, the questionnaire used technical language such as “algorithmic transparency” and “predictive latency” without explanation. For busy clinicians with varying digital literacy, this increases the risk of confusion and unreliable responses.

The ordering and grouping of questions also lacked structure. Items about usability, trust, training and perceived risk were mixed together without headings or logical flow. This can cause respondent fatigue and encourage satisficing (ticking answers quickly rather than thinking carefully).

In the wiki, I suggested splitting double-barrelled items, simplifying terminology with short clarifications, and organising questions into themed sections with clear instructions. I also recommended including a small number of open-ended questions to capture concerns that fixed scales might miss. This activity reinforced how good questionnaire design is not just about aesthetics, but about clarity, fairness and alignment with the research aims.

Unit 5: Reflective Activity – Inappropriate Use of Surveys

Recognising bias, poor fit, and ethical risks in survey design

I completed the reflective activity on inappropriate use of surveys. I explored how surveys can be misused when they are poorly aligned to the research question, contain leading or ambiguous items, or encourage biased interpretations.

Key points

  • Surveys should not be used to “prove” a desired outcome.
  • Poor wording and unclear constructs reduce validity and reliability.
  • Ethical risks increase when data collection is excessive or consent is unclear.

Reflection

This activity reinforced that research tools must be chosen and designed based on fit, fairness, and transparency — especially in healthcare contexts where results can influence decisions and policy.
